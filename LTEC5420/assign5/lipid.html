Biomolecules

Lipids

Lipids: Key Facts

  • Key Elements: C, H, O (Not a 1:2:1 Ratio)
  • Monomer forms: Fatty Acids and Glycerol
  • Polymer forms: Triglycerides, Steroids, Phospholipids
  • Functions: A major energy storage molecule, insulation (keep warm in winter), main part of the cell membrane
  • Additional facts: Two major forms of lipids are saturated fats (usually solid at room temperature, and unsaturated fats (usually liquid)
  • Fats are hydrophobic. They do not mix with water
  • Common forms: Fats, Oils, and waxes
  • 60 percent of your brain is made of fat.
  • Every cell in your body is surrounded by fat
  • While protein and carbohydrates contain 4 calories per gram, fat provides 9 calories.

Lipid Saturation
  • The fatty acids contain differing amounts of hydrogen atoms. A "full" fatty acid is saturated, while those with incomplete amounts of hydrogen are "unsaturated"
  • Because of their shape, Lipids that are saturated are solid at room temperature while unsaturated fats tend to be lipids
  • Unsaturated fats get their name based on the location they are missing hydrogen molecules. Omega-3 fatty acids are missing a hydrogen at the third position, while omega-6 are missing a hydrogen at the sixth.
  • Transfats are unsaturated fats that are not shaped in a naturally occuring shape. They are solid at room temperature, and are considered extremely unhealthy
