Overview

Nielsen’s five usability criteria

  • Efficiency: Users accomplish their tasks quickly and without much cognitive effort (Leuthold, Bargas-Avila, & Opwis, 2008)
  • Errors: Users commit only few errors and are able to recover quickly (Leuthold, Bargas-Avila, & Opwis, 2008)
  • Satisfaction: Users are satisfied with how the website works (Leuthold, Bargas-Avila, & Opwis, 2008)
  • Memorability: Returning users do not have to relearn the use of navigation items and functionalities (Leuthold, Bargas-Avila, & Opwis, 2008)
  • Learnability: Novice users of a website get productive quickly, finding information and using the website’s functionality (Leuthold, Bargas-Avila, & Opwis, 2008)

More Information

  • In a study of sites between 1997 to 2002 using the internet archive site Wayback machine, it was “found that websites became progressively inaccessible, whereas their complexity increased”. (Leuthold, Bargas-Avila, & Opwis, 2008)
  • In a similar study in 2012, it was found that approximately 51% of government sites mentioned the term accessibility in their websites whereas only 14 percent of “top websites” did (Hanson & Richards, 2013)
  • It would seem that accessibly indicators that are easy to implement and to detect by algorithms have improved while “deeper analysis of accessibility indicators ... may reveal more accessibility problems” (Hanson & Richards, 2013)
  • “government sites had fewer accessibility problems than did topsites.” (Hanson & Richards, 2013)
  • “Specifically, these pages exhibited improved accessibility as a side-effect of the use of new browser features to enhance page layout and design, page coding for optimization of page rank in search results, and changes in coding to attain both better cross-browser consistency and cross-device compatibility.” (Hanson & Richards, 2013)
  • “At least some improvements appear to be side-effects of good coding practices and the desire to improve Web page design and website prominence in search results.” (Hanson & Richards, 2013)
  • “Fixing a problem after a product is released is 10 times more costly than when the same problem is fixed during the development, and 100 times more costly than when it is fixed during the design.” (Loiacono & Djamasbi, 2011)
